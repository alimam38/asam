## 1. The Sixth District / Georgia small-business credit picture

The Atlanta Fed compiles regional profiles from the national Small Business Credit Survey (SBCS), covering the Sixth Federal Reserve District (Alabama, Florida, Georgia, and portions of Louisiana, Mississippi, Tennessee) with specific cuts for Georgia and the Atlanta–Sandy Springs–Roswell MSA. [T1 — Atlanta Fed / Federal Reserve SBCS]

**The single most important regional finding:**

Compared to US firms overall, Sixth District firms were **more likely to apply for financing** in the prior 12 months but **less likely to receive partial or full approval.** [T1 — Atlanta Fed, 2025 Southeast Insights, drawing on 2024 SBCS]

That one sentence is the local validation of the entire thesis: demand is higher here and fulfillment is lower here. The gap the institution exists to close is measurably wider in its launch market than in the country as a whole.

**Regional financial condition** [T1 — Atlanta Fed, 2025 Southeast Insights]:
- **60% of Sixth District firms reported poor or fair financial conditions** — a majority of the regional small-business base describing itself as financially strained.

**Georgia-specific challenges**, 2024 SBCS data (Atlanta Fed Georgia Insights) [T1]:
- Increased costs of goods/services/wages — 75%
- Paying operating expenses — 56%
- Uneven cash flow — 49%
- Weak sales — 42%
- Credit availability — 39%
- Making payments on debt / interest rates — 32%

Uneven cash flow as a top-cited challenge is notable: it is precisely the condition conventional underwriting penalizes and that a relationship-based, behavior-reading underwriting model is designed to serve.

**Atlanta–Sandy Springs–Roswell MSA specifics**, 2024 SBCS data (Atlanta Fed Atlanta Insights, N=150 metro firms) [T1] — *now retrieved in full; this is the metro-specific cut v1 was missing*:
- **56% of Atlanta firms reported poor or fair financial conditions** in 2024 (up from 53% in 2023) — marginally below the 60% district figure, but still a majority.
- **33% reported operating at a loss** at the end of 2024 (up from 31%).
- **The financing application rate rose from 37% (2023) to 42% (2024)** — credit demand is climbing year-over-year in the launch metro itself.
- Metro financial challenges track the state: increased costs 71%, paying operating expenses 59% (up sharply from 49%), uneven cash flow 46%, weak sales 41%, debt/interest payments 37%, credit availability 32%.

**The standout metro finding — the behavioral signal:** among Atlanta firms that sought financing in 2024, the most-sought source was **credit cards at 43%, up from 25% the year before** — a 18-point jump in a single year. Business loans were sought by 30% and lines of credit by 28%. A small-business base increasingly putting its financing on credit cards — the most expensive and least durable form of capital — is the misbanking dynamic captured in behavior: capable operators routing to suboptimal capital because proper credit isn't reaching them. (The Fed's "Loan" category here explicitly includes mortgage/real-estate and auto/equipment loans — i.e., the institution's likely first product lines sit inside the 30% loan-application figure.) *Caveat: the Atlanta MSA sample is N=150 and the SBCS is a convenience sample; the Fed flags that not all year-over-year moves are statistically significant. Treat the credit-card jump as a strong directional signal, not a precise point estimate.*

Demographically, the Atlanta metro firm base skews toward exactly the target operator [T1, same sheet]: **32% owned by people of color** (vs. 29% statewide), 24% women-owned, **41% of firms are 0–5 years old** (thin-file by conventional standards), and **60% have just 1–4 employees** — the micro-operator profile that conventional underwriting reads poorly.

**National context that frames the regional gap** [T1 — Fed SBCS, 2026 Report on Employer Firms, 2025 survey data]:
- 59% of firms sought new financing in the prior 12 months; 40% of applicants sought less than $50,000 — a small-ticket, high-volume credit demand profile.
- The share of firms applying at online/fintech lenders rose for the fifth consecutive year: **17% (2020) → 29% (2025).**
- Applicants at small banks were the most likely to be **fully approved (57%)** — more than online lenders or large banks.
- Net satisfaction with online lenders collapsed from **15% to 2%** between the 2023 and 2024 surveys; high interest rates and unfavorable repayment terms were the most common complaints at online lenders.

Approval rates by lender type from the same survey cycle, for sizing the competitive field [T3 — secondary compilation of 2024 SBCS data]: small banks ~52%, credit unions ~51%, finance companies ~51%, large banks ~44%, online lenders ~31%.

**The opening this documents:** operators are routing to online/fintech lenders out of necessity, in rising numbers, and getting surprised by cost and terms — satisfaction with that channel has nearly vanished. A membership institution that reads them accurately, prices fairly, and holds the relationship addresses a dissatisfaction the federal data already documents.

## 2. The mortgage-denial mechanism — DTI and the misbanked borrower (NEW)

This section is the core upgrade in v2. The gap data (Section 1) shows *that* the misbanked are underserved. This section shows the federal data on *why* — and the why is the thesis.

### The national mechanism

[T1 — Federal Reserve Bank of Atlanta, "What Do Home Mortgage Disclosure Act Data Tell Us about the Southeast?", Nov. 5, 2025, by Sakar Prasain — analysis of 2024 HMDA loan-level data]

- About **24% of applicants nationally who received a decision were denied** a mortgage in 2024 (down 1.3 points from 2023).
- **The leading reasons for denial are debt-to-income (DTI) ratios and credit history** — DTI standing out across the board (the article's Figure 3).
- A general association between higher DTI and denial holds **across all racial groups** (Figure 4) — i.e., DTI is the structural gate, not a proxy effect isolated to one population.

Why this is the whole argument: **DTI is precisely the metric that misreads the misbanked borrower.** A self-employed operator who writes off expenses, a 1099 contractor, a real-estate investor whose income is in distributions and depreciation, a business owner whose money runs through the company — all of them present a distorted DTI to a conventional underwriter even when their genuine capacity to pay is strong. The single most common reason mortgages are denied in America is the exact measurement error the institution exists to correct. This is no longer an inference from a gap; it is the federal data naming the failure mode.

### The Southeast carries the heaviest denial load in the country

[T1 — same Atlanta Fed HMDA analysis]
- **The Southeast (Sixth District) had the highest mortgage-denial count of all 12 Federal Reserve districts in 2024: about 404,000 denials — 20% of all denials nationally — versus an average of about 145,000 for the other 11 districts.** Part of this is high application volume, but the Fed explicitly frames the denial rate as speaking to "the heightened relevance of mortgage availability in this region, as well as the need to ensure access."
- Sixth District monthly mortgage payments run about **40% of area median income**, versus about 45% nationally — affordability is strained, though modestly less than the national figure.
- Payment-to-income (PTI) ratios have been **hovering between 40% and 50%** over the last two years; a PTI above 30% is generally considered unaffordable.
- Total loan costs rose sharply in 2022 for almost every district, and **most steeply for the Sixth District — a ~40% increase** — the highest jump of any Fed district.

### The Fed names the opening, in its own analysis

The same article observes that the market share of loan originations and applications **originating outside traditional banks has remained stable, and that nontraditional/alternative lending "appears to be on a path of sustained growth."** It goes on to flag, for regulators, the need to watch these lenders' "tolerance for riskier metrics" as competition widens. Read plainly: the Federal Reserve's own regional analysis identifies non-traditional lenders growing into exactly the affordability-and-access gap this institution targets, and treats that growth as a structural trend rather than a blip. That is unusually direct third-party validation of the market's direction.

### The Atlanta-MSA-specific denial picture

[T2 — Urban Institute, "Atlanta Market: Keys Unlock Dreams Initiative," 2019 HMDA purchase-loan data]
- For applicants denied a purchase mortgage in Atlanta, **DTI was the most frequently cited reason — more than 25% of denied Black applicants in the city and 33% in the MSA were denied because of DTI.** DTI accounts for a much higher share of denials for Black applicants than for any other group.
- A high share of Atlanta applicants were also denied for **incomplete applications** — flagged by the authors as warranting further examination (and, notably, a failure mode that a high-touch, relationship-based originator is well-positioned to prevent).
- Mortgage demand among Black households is itself suppressed: in the city of Atlanta, **only 1.4% of Black households applied for a purchase mortgage, versus 3% of white households** — the application gap precedes the denial gap.

[T2 — LendingTree analysis of 2024 HMDA data]
- Nationally in 2024, **failing the DTI requirement accounted for 34.02% of all denials and 34.08% of denials for Black borrowers** — DTI is the number-one obstacle, with credit history second.
- **Atlanta had the second-highest share of Black mortgage applicants among the 50 largest metros (25.35%)**, behind only Memphis — i.e., the misbanked-adjacent applicant pool is unusually large here.
- Atlanta's Black-vs-overall denial-rate gap was about 4.01 percentage points, close to the 50-metro average of 4.80.

[T2 — Reinvestment Fund, June 2025, analysis of Fulton, DeKalb, Cobb, Gwinnett, and Clayton counties]
- Across the Atlanta region, applicants of color were more likely to be denied conventional loans than white applicants **with similar financial characteristics and histories.**
- **Higher DTI and loan-to-value above 90% were among the significant drivers** of denial, alongside lower incomes. The disparity persisted even among well-qualified applicants. Builds on the Fund's 2024 "10 Southeastern Cities" Black–white mortgage-gap report.

[T2/T3 — Georgia Tech, Atlanta metro HMDA analysis 2012–2017]
- The largest barriers to approval for Black and Latino applicants were **credit history, DTI, insufficient funds, employment history, and incomplete applications** — the same cluster, a decade earlier, showing this is structural and persistent rather than a recent artifact.

**What this section establishes:** Four independent sources — the Atlanta Fed (T1), the Urban Institute, LendingTree, and the Reinvestment Fund — converge on the same finding from different angles and different years. DTI is the dominant mortgage-denial mechanism nationally and in Atlanta specifically; it is the exact metric that misreads complex-income operators; the Southeast carries the heaviest denial load in the country; and the disparities persist even among financially comparable applicants. The institution's underwriting thesis is the direct answer to a federally-documented, locally-concentrated, decade-persistent measurement failure.

## 3. The Atlanta homeownership gap — the housing side of the thesis

[T2 — Urban Institute, Atlanta "Keys Unlock Dreams" chartbook]
- More than 75% of white households in the MSA were homeowners (2015–19).
- The Black homeownership rate in the MSA *declined* from 53% to 47% between 2005–09 and 2015–19 — the steepest decline of any group, slow to recover from the Great Recession.
- Black households make up 45% of households in the city of Atlanta and 33% in the MSA — a large population on the wrong side of the widest gap.
- A rising **cash-buyer share** in the Atlanta MSA has been re-entrenching the wealth losses Black communities suffered in the foreclosure crisis — those who can't access mortgage credit are being outbid by cash.

[T2 — JPMorgan Chase Institute, "Housing affordability worsens in Atlanta," Nov. 2025]
- The monthly mortgage expense for would-be Atlanta homebuyers rose from **25% of median income in 2019 to 41% in 2025** — homeownership costs up over 60% relative to incomes in six years.

[T2/T3 — Census ACS / regional data]
- City of Atlanta homeownership rate ~46.4% (2024), well below the national ~65%; MSA ~65.8%. Median property value: city ~$439,600, MSA ~$335,100 (2023–24).

**What this establishes:** the homeownership product side faces a market where the racial gap is wide and widening, affordability has deteriorated sharply, cash buyers are displacing credit-dependent buyers, and the misbanked operator is exactly the buyer most likely to be squeezed out by DTI-driven underwriting even when they can genuinely afford the home. Non-QM / bank-statement / DSCR products are the direct instrument for that buyer.

## 4. The non-QM market — recalibrated and honestly bounded (REVISED)

v1 (and the parent read) carried a non-QM market size of roughly $239B / ~10% of originations. The diligence pass found that figure should be **recalibrated downward and, more importantly, presented as a range, because non-QM has no standardized definition** — a caveat that matters for capital-grade credibility.

### The definitional problem (state this before any number)

[T2 — Polygon Research methodology, Jan. 2026]
HMDA contains **no standardized non-QM indicator.** Every market-size figure in circulation is an *estimate* derived from securitization data, lender classifications, or inferred assumptions, and different vendors use different definitions. Any number presented to capital should be presented with its methodology, not as a settled fact. This is the single most important credibility point in the section.

### The range, by source

- **~$182 billion / ~9% of total originations in 2024** — Polygon Research's loan-level estimate, built by classifying every 2018–2024 HMDA record against the ATR/QM framework (12 CFR §1026.43). [T2 — Polygon, the most methodologically transparent of the estimates]
- **~5% of originations in 2024, up from 3% in 2020** — Cotality data, via Scotsman Guide. [T2/T3]
- Production roughly **10% above 2019 levels** — Inside Mortgage Finance. [T3]
- Origination volume grew **more than 20% year-over-year in 2024**, even as the overall mortgage market sat ~20% below pre-pandemic levels — non-QM is the segment bucking the broader slump. [T2/T3 — Scotsman Guide]

Honest framing for a deck: *"Non-QM is estimated at roughly 5–9% of 2024 originations (~$182B on the most transparent loan-level methodology), with no standardized definition across sources; what's consistent across every source is the growth direction — non-QM is expanding while the broader mortgage market contracts."*

### The finding that matters most for the thesis

[T2/T3 — Scotsman Guide, Nov. 2025]
**The average non-QM borrower carried a 776 FICO in 2024 — essentially on par with conventional conforming borrowers.** This directly dispels the subprime stigma: the non-QM borrower is not a credit risk, but a *documentation-and-structure* mismatch. That is the misbanked thesis stated in a single data point — financially capable, simply outside the conventional box.

### The product scope confirms the MVP is in-market

[T2 — Polygon methodology]
**DSCR and business-purpose investor loans are counted within non-QM** (flagged separately, since ATR/QM rules don't govern business-purpose credit). The five borrower types that dominate non-QM pipelines — self-employed (bank-statement / 1099), real-estate investors (DSCR), and adjacent profiles — map precisely onto the institution's target segment and the likely first MVP product (business-purpose RE / DSCR).

### Competitive intelligence (validates demand; signals the clock)

- **Angel Oak** — the dominant non-QM specialist — is **headquartered in Atlanta**: $10B+ lifetime non-QM origination, $3.9B in 2021 alone. The launch market is also the home market of the category's biggest specialist. That cuts both ways: it proves Atlanta sustains non-QM at scale, and it means the institution is not differentiating on product access but on the *membership architecture, relationship underwriting, and member-development model* around the product. [T2/T3 — company releases]
- **PennyMac launched a full non-QM TPO suite in March 2026** — DSCR, bank-statement, asset-qualifier/depletion, 1099, WVOE, full-doc-for-nontraditional-income — explicitly to serve "self-employed, entrepreneurs, and business owners." A top-tier national lender entering this segment *now* validates the demand and signals that the window for a differentiated position is open but not indefinite. [T2/T3 — company release]

**The strategic read:** product access to non-QM is commoditizing (PennyMac, Angel Oak, and others are all in). That is *not* where the institution wins. The defensible position is everything the incumbents don't do — the membership architecture, the relationship-held underwriting that reads the misbanked borrower correctly, and the development model that moves members toward mainstream bankability while the institution retains the relationship and the portfolio intelligence.

That is the institution's launch thesis stated in federal and institutional data.

[T2/T3 — Invest Atlanta, ACE, ANDP, press reporting]

- **ANDP** — "Closing the Gap" plan targeting thousands of single-family and affordable units, backed by corporate/foundation capital.

**The structural observation:** every one is nonprofit, CDFI, grant-dependent, or foundation-backed. They prove the demand and the gap beyond dispute. None is a for-profit, membership-architected institution that develops members toward bankability, holds the relationship, and turns portfolio performance into capital leverage with mainstream banks. The philanthropic/CDFI infrastructure validates the need; it does not occupy the position. That is the whitespace, locally confirmed.
