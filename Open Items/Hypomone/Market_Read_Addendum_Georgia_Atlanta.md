## Why this layer matters

The parent market read established the national segment and named Georgia as a strong launch geography. This addendum goes deeper into the Atlanta–Sandy Springs–Roswell MSA specifically, because that is where the institution would launch and where Keena, her network, and the founding cohort actually operate. The local data is, if anything, *more* favorable to the thesis than the national data — and it comes from unusually credible sources: the Atlanta Fed's own regional small-business analysis, the Urban Institute's Atlanta homeownership chartbook, and the JPMorgan Chase Institute's 2025 Atlanta housing affordability study.

The throughline of this layer: metro Atlanta concentrates exactly the conditions the institution is built for — a large and growing base of self-employed operators and investors, a documented and widening homeownership gap, a small-business credit market where local firms apply *more* but get approved *less*, and an incumbent response that is real but nonprofit-scaled and grant-dependent.

## 1. The Sixth District / Georgia small-business credit picture

The Atlanta Fed compiles regional profiles from the national Small Business Credit Survey, covering the Sixth Federal Reserve District (Alabama, Florida, Georgia, and portions of Louisiana, Mississippi, Tennessee) with specific cuts for Georgia and the Atlanta–Sandy Springs–Roswell MSA. [T1 — Atlanta Fed / Federal Reserve SBCS]

**The single most important regional finding:**

Compared to US firms overall, Sixth District firms were **more likely to apply for financing** in the prior 12 months but **less likely to receive partial or full approval.** [T1 — Atlanta Fed, 2025 Southeast Insights, drawing on 2024 SBCS]

That one sentence is the local validation of the entire thesis. The demand is higher here and the fulfillment is lower here. The gap the institution exists to close is measurably wider in its launch market than in the country as a whole.

**Georgia-specific challenges** reported by firms in the 2024 SBCS data (Atlanta Fed Georgia Insights): [T1]
- Uneven cash flow — 49% of Georgia firms
- Weak sales — 42%
- Credit availability — 39%
- Making payments on debt / interest rates — 32%

Uneven cash flow as the single most-cited challenge is notable: it is precisely the condition that conventional underwriting penalizes and that a relationship-based, behavior-reading underwriting model is designed to serve.

**National context that frames the regional gap** [T1 — Fed SBCS 2026 Report on Employer Firms]:
- About half of firms had their funding needs fully met; about one-third faced a funding gap despite applying.
- The share of firms applying at online/fintech lenders rose for the fifth consecutive year (17% in 2020 → 29% in 2025).
- Among firms that borrowed from online lenders, 60% reported higher-than-expected borrowing costs — meaningfully more than borrowers from other lender types.

That last point is a direct opening: operators are routing to online lenders out of necessity and getting surprised by cost. A membership institution that reads them accurately and prices fairly addresses a dissatisfaction the federal data already documents.

## 2. The Atlanta homeownership gap — the housing side of the thesis

This is where the local data is most striking, and where the most credible sources converge.

**The racial homeownership gap in metro Atlanta** [T2 — Urban Institute, Atlanta "Keys Unlock Dreams" chartbook]:
- More than 75% of white households in the MSA were homeowners (2015–19).
- The Black homeownership rate in the MSA *declined* from 53% to 47% between 2005–09 and 2015–19 — the steepest decline of any group, and slow to recover from the Great Recession.
- Black households make up 45% of households in the city of Atlanta and 33% in the MSA — a large population on the wrong side of the widest gap.
- A greater share of Black households are housing-cost-burdened (paying 30%+ of income on housing), especially renters.
- The report specifically notes that a rising **cash-buyer share** in the Atlanta MSA has been re-entrenching the wealth losses Black communities suffered in the foreclosure crisis — i.e., those who can't access mortgage credit are being outbid by cash.

**Affordability deterioration** [T2 — JPMorgan Chase Institute, "Housing affordability worsens in Atlanta," Nov. 2025]:
- The monthly mortgage expense for would-be Atlanta homebuyers rose from 25% of median income in 2019 to 41% in 2025 — homeownership costs up over 60% relative to incomes in six years.

**Market structure** [T2/T3 — Atlanta Regional Commission / Federal Reserve / Redfin data]:
- As 30-year rates climbed from pandemic lows near 2.5% to peaks above 7%, the qualified-buyer pool shrank materially: homes selling within two weeks fell from over 50% (2021–22) to roughly one in seven by late 2024, recovering only to ~30% in early 2025.
- City of Atlanta homeownership rate: 46.4% (2024), well below the national ~65%. MSA homeownership rate: ~65.8%. [T2 — Census ACS via Data USA] Median property value: city ~$439,600, MSA ~$335,100 (2023–24).

**What this establishes:** The homeownership product side of the institution faces a market where (a) the racial gap is wide and widening, (b) affordability has deteriorated sharply, (c) cash buyers are displacing credit-dependent buyers, and (d) the misbanked operator — self-employed, complex-income, investor — is exactly the buyer most likely to be squeezed out by conventional DTI-driven underwriting even when they can genuinely afford the home. Non-QM / bank-statement / DSCR products are the direct instrument for that buyer, and Georgia is the fastest-growing non-QM market in the country (per the parent read: ~21,164 non-QM originations in 2024, +27.7% YoY; ~230,000 potential non-QM borrowers statewide).

## 3. The local entrepreneurial density

Metro Atlanta is not just underserved — it is densely populated with exactly the operators the institution serves.

[T2 — LendingTree analysis of Census Annual Business Survey; National Equity Atlas]
- Atlanta has the highest share of Black-owned businesses among the 50 largest US metros for four consecutive years — 7.4% of metro businesses.
- ~7%+ of regional businesses were Black-owned as of 2021, among the highest rates nationwide.
- 39.3% of Black-owned businesses nationally are women-led, a rising share — relevant to the inclusion framing and to Keena's own positioning.

[T1 — Census] Georgia ranks among the most entrepreneurially active states, with the bulk of nonemployer and employer business activity concentrated in metro Atlanta.

**The convergence:** the densest concentration of the target operator in the country, sitting inside the widest and fastest-deteriorating homeownership gap, in the fastest-growing non-QM market. That is not a coincidence the institution has to argue around — it is the institution's launch thesis stated in federal and institutional data.

## 4. The incumbent response — real, but leaving the position open

The local capital-access gap has drawn responses, which prove demand while leaving the membership-architected, performance-funded position unoccupied.

[T2/T3 — Invest Atlanta, ACE, press reporting]
- **Access to Capital for Entrepreneurs (ACE)** — CDFI serving 68 Georgia counties; $37M+ in loans in a recent year, 94% to underserved entrepreneurs; participant in a $100M JPMorgan Chase Entrepreneurs of Color commitment.
- **Invest Atlanta / Atlanta Open for Business Fund** — 200+ small businesses supported across multiple funds; 85.6% of funded businesses minority-owned, 78.8% Black-owned, 66% women-owned. Invest Atlanta explicitly cites the national gap: ~35% of white business owners received all financing applied for vs. ~16% of Black business owners.
- **ANDP (Atlanta Neighborhood Development Partnership)** — "Closing the Gap" plan targeting thousands of single-family homes and affordable units, backed by corporate/foundation capital (e.g., Regions Foundation).

**The structural observation:** every one of these is nonprofit, CDFI, grant-dependent, or foundation-backed. They prove the demand and the gap beyond dispute. What none of them is: a for-profit, membership-architected institution that develops members toward bankability, holds the relationship, and turns portfolio performance into capital leverage with mainstream banks. The philanthropic and CDFI infrastructure validates the need; it does not occupy the position you're describing. That is the whitespace, locally confirmed.

## 5. What this local layer adds to the Keena update

**Three sentences you can stand behind, each federally or institutionally sourced:**

1. "In metro Atlanta, small firms apply for financing at higher rates than the national average but are approved at lower rates" — Atlanta Fed, SBCS. [T1]

2. "Black homeownership in the Atlanta MSA fell from 53% to 47% over the period studied while white homeownership held above 75%, and mortgage costs have risen from 25% to 41% of median income since 2019" — Urban Institute and JPMorgan Chase Institute. [T2]

3. "Atlanta has the highest concentration of Black-owned businesses among the 50 largest US metros, four years running, and Georgia is the fastest-growing non-QM mortgage market in the country" — LendingTree/Census and Polygon/HMDA. [T2]

**The framing those three sentences support:** the launch market isn't chosen for convenience or affinity. It's chosen because metro Atlanta is, on the federal and institutional data, the single most concentrated overlap in the country of (a) the target operator, (b) the documented credit and homeownership gap, and (c) market growth — with an incumbent response that proves demand without occupying the position.
