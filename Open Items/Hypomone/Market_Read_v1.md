## How to read this document

Every claim below is tagged by source tier so you can defend it — or discount it — in a room with someone who evaluates this for a living.

- **[T1]** — Primary government or central-bank source (FDIC, Federal Reserve, Census, Treasury, CFPB, state statute). Citable without qualification.
- **[T2]** — Credible institutional research (Federal Reserve Bank working papers, Urban Institute, academic studies, established market-data firms with disclosed methodology). Citable with attribution.
- **[T3]** — Industry/commercial sources (lender marketing, trade press, comparison sites). Directional only — used here to characterize the competitive field, never as evidence of market size or need. Flagged explicitly where used.

A note on what this document is and isn't: this is a **secondary-source synthesis** — it establishes that the segment exists, is large, and is underserved, using others' data. It does not replace the **first-party evidence** your own survey will generate. The two work together: this read sizes the opportunity; the survey proves demand within your specific network and produces the proprietary data that becomes your institutional sales asset.

## 1. The segment, defined precisely

The customer base is not the "unbanked" or "underbanked" population as the FDIC defines those terms. That distinction matters, because conflating them would undercut your credibility immediately.

The FDIC's framework: a household is **unbanked** if no one has a checking or savings account, and **underbanked** if banked but also using high-cost alternative services (payday loans, check cashing, pawnshops, auto-title loans). [T1] In 2023, the national unbanked rate fell to 4.3%, a historical low, while the underbanked rate sat at 14.2%. [T1]

Your segment is different and more specific: **financially capable operators whose income or business structure doesn't map cleanly onto conventional underwriting.** They have accounts. They have assets. They have real cash flow. They are not using payday lenders. They are "misbanked" — served by institutions whose underwriting models can't read them — which is a category the federal surveys don't name directly but the lending data captures clearly.

The bridge concept that does appear in the research: the Federal Reserve Bank of New York's 2025 financial-inclusion review distinguishes "credit invisible" consumers (no credit history with major bureaus) from "credit unscorable" individuals — those whose credit history is too limited or non-standard to produce a conventional score "even in cases where the person has a long history of bill payments not traditionally reported to credit bureaus, such as for rent and utilities." [T2] Your segment is the higher-income analog of that unscorable problem: people whose *actual* financial behavior is strong but whose *documented, conventionally-modeled* profile reads as risk.

## 2. Segment size — national

**Self-employed Americans:** As of December 2025, an estimated 16.63 million Americans were self-employed (6.94M incorporated, 9.69M unincorporated), roughly 10.2% of the civilian labor force, per analysis of the Census/BLS Current Population Survey. [T2, drawing on T1 CPS data]

**Business owners more broadly:** The Census Bureau counted 30.4 million nonemployer businesses operating in 2023, and a Census working paper puts the total population of business owners and self-employed at roughly 33 million when accounting for overlaps and incorporated owners. [T1] There were an additional ~8.3 million employer businesses in 2022. [T1]

**The non-QM mortgage market** — the clearest proxy for the lending demand your segment represents:

- Non-QM loans represented approximately 9.0% of total U.S. mortgage originations in 2024 by loan count (~$182 billion across ~559,000 loans), rising to ~10% in 2025 (~$239 billion across ~698,000 loans), per Polygon Research's classification of loan-level HMDA data. [T2 — market-data firm with disclosed HMDA-based methodology]
- DSCR loans (the real estate investor subset) account for roughly 28–29% of non-QM originations and a striking ~52% of non-QM securitization volume as of mid-2025. [T3 — trade source; treat the securitization figure as directional]

The non-QM figure is the single most useful market-size anchor you have, because it is derived from HMDA — a [T1] federal disclosure dataset — and it measures actual originations to exactly your two segments: self-employed borrowers (bank statement / P&L / asset-utilization loans) and real estate investors (DSCR / business-purpose loans). It is demonstrated demand, not estimated demand.

## 3. The underservice — quantified

This is the heart of the validation: the segment isn't just large, it's measurably underserved.

**Mortgage denial, broadly:** In 2024, lenders denied more than 526,000 home-purchase applications — about one in six. The 2022–2023 monetary tightening drove aggregate denial rates from 12.2% to 15.7%, and the mechanism was specifically the **debt-to-income (DTI) channel**, per a St. Louis Fed analysis of 30M+ HMDA applications. [T2, on T1 data] DTI is precisely the metric that misreads a self-employed operator whose tax-optimized AGI understates real cash flow.

**The self-employed underwriting penalty:** Conventional underwriting evaluates net income after deductions. As the lending field itself describes it, this net figure is "too low to qualify for a conventional mortgage, even if their actual cash flow is incredibly strong." [T3 — lender source, used only to characterize the mechanism, which is well-established] Manual underwriting data indicates self-employed borrowers are disproportionately routed out of automated approval. [T3]

**Small business credit denial:** The Federal Reserve's Small Business Credit Survey is the gold standard here.
- Among small businesses that sought financing, roughly half received the full amount requested. [T2 — Fed SBCS]
- The share of applicants seeking financing from online/fintech lenders rose from 17% (2020) to 29% (2025) — a direct signal that conventional banks are not meeting this demand and operators are routing around them. [T1 — Fed SBCS 2026 Report on Employer Firms]
- Applicant satisfaction with lenders **decreased** year over year even as approval rates held steady. [T1 — Fed SBCS]

**The demographic dimension** (relevant given the inclusion framing you and Keena are building around):
- Federal Reserve SBCS data has consistently found that firms owned by people of color are less than half as likely as comparable white-owned firms to receive full financing — and that this holds *even when credit risk is comparable.* In the benchmark 2021 analysis, low-credit-risk Black- and Latino-owned firms were approved for full financing at rates close to *high/medium*-risk white-owned firms. [T1 — Federal Reserve Banks SBCS / Report on Firms Owned by People of Color]
- National averages cited by Invest Atlanta: ~35% of white business owners received all the financing they applied for, versus ~16% of Black business owners. [T2/T3 — economic development authority citing Fed data]

The throughline: this is not a population that fails on the merits. It is a population that fails on the *model*. That distinction is the entire thesis of the institution, and it is supported by federal data rather than by assertion.

## 4. Segment size and underservice — Georgia and the Southeast

This is where the launch case sharpens, and where the data is unusually favorable.

**Georgia non-QM market:**
- In 2024, lenders originated approximately 21,164 non-QM loans in Georgia, a 27.7% year-over-year increase — one of the fastest-growing non-QM markets in the country. [T2 — Polygon Research / HMDA]
- Polygon estimates Georgia contains approximately **230,493 potential non-QM borrowers**, comprising roughly **109,986 self-employed households** and **120,507 small real estate investors.** [T2]
- Activity concentrates in metro Atlanta, with additional volume in Savannah and Augusta. [T2]
- Georgia ranks ninth nationally for total self-employed workers, with concentration in sales and professional services. [T3 — lender citing Census data; the ranking is directional]

**Atlanta's distinctive position:**
- Atlanta has, for four consecutive years, the **highest share of Black-owned businesses** among the 50 largest U.S. metros — 7.4% of metro businesses, up from 6.7% the prior year. [T2 — LendingTree analysis of Census Annual Business Survey data]
- 39.3% of Black-owned businesses nationally are headed by women, a rising share. [T2 — same source]
- Slightly more than 7% of regional Atlanta businesses were Black-owned as of 2021, one of the highest rates nationwide. [T2 — National Equity Atlas, on Census data]

**The Atlanta capital-access gap (the local proof of underservice):**
- Business loan denial rates for firms owned by people of color in the Atlanta context run more than twice as high as for white-owned businesses, with higher interest rates and smaller loan/equity amounts when approved. [T2 — National Equity Atlas]
- The existing institutional response is CDFI-led: Access to Capital for Entrepreneurs (ACE), serving 68 Georgia counties, made $37M+ in loans in a recent year with 94% to underserved entrepreneurs, and participates in a $100M JPMorgan Chase Entrepreneurs of Color commitment. [T2/T3 — nonprofit and press reporting]

The Georgia/Atlanta read is the strongest single argument for launch geography: a fast-growing non-QM market, the nation's densest concentration of Black-owned businesses, a measurable and documented capital-access gap, and an existing CDFI infrastructure that proves demand but operates at nonprofit scale and grant-dependent capital — leaving the *membership-architected, performance-funded* model you're describing as an unoccupied position.

## 5. The competitive field

Characterized here using a mix of tiers; nothing in this section is offered as proof of market need (Sections 2–4 do that), only as a map of who currently operates.

**Non-QM mortgage lenders** [T3 — lender sources]: Deephaven, Carrington, LendingOne, Truss Financial, Capital Home Mortgage, FNBA, AD Mortgage, and others actively serve Georgia with bank-statement, DSCR, asset-utilization, and P&L products. The product category is mature and competitive. **None operate as membership institutions**; the relationship is transactional and ends at closing.

**Real estate investor lenders** [T3]: Kiavi, Easy Street Capital, Lima One, Insula, LendingOne, Conventus. Mature DSCR/fix-and-flip/bridge product lines. Same structural gap — lender-to-borrower transactions, no community architecture, no member development.

**Modern fintech banking** [T3]: Mercury, Brex (being acquired by Capital One, ~$5.15B, expected to close mid-2026), Ramp, Relay, Slash. Strong design, transparency, and "built for operators" posture — but oriented to venture-backed startups, not the misbanked operator running a portfolio or service business.

**Credit unions** [T1/T2]: The original membership-based financial institution — member-owned, not-for-profit, tax-advantaged. The structural form you're drawing on. Their documented weakness is narrative and visibility: research shows credit unions "win in almost every category once consumers join" but are "barely seen as best at anything" beforehand. [T3 — industry research] The form works; the cultural posture for your target cohort generally doesn't.

**Private members' clubs** [T2/T3]: Soho House (taken private at ~$2.7B enterprise value in 2025, having never posted a profit since 1995), Core Club, Zero Bond. The community/belonging architecture you're referencing — but the pure social-capital model is under strain, and the clubs adapting successfully now generate 30–40% of revenue from business services rather than exclusivity alone. [T3] This validates the *members-club-plus-real-utility* model and cautions against members-club-as-status-alone.

**The unoccupied position:** No competitor combines (a) the financial-product depth of a non-QM lender, (b) the community/belonging architecture of a members' club, and (c) the member-development discipline of a credit union's "we win once you join" pattern. The combination is the whitespace.

## 6. Regulatory pathway — with your NMLS standing factored in

This is the section where your existing credential changes the analysis materially.

**The general rule** [T1 — CSBS, state statute]: States license non-bank financial activity on an "activities-based" rather than "entity-based" basis. Consumer lending, mortgage origination, and money transmission each trigger separate licensing. Multistate licensing runs through the NMLS. Licensing can take many months to years per state.

**Georgia mortgage licensing specifics** [T1 — O.C.G.A. § 7-1-1000 et seq.; GA Dept. of Banking and Finance Rules 80-11-4 and 80-11-5]:
- Residential mortgage origination in Georgia requires licensure through NMLS, with the SAFE Act 20-hour coursework, testing, fingerprinting, and an office of record.
- Mortgage *lender* (as opposed to originator) licensure requires demonstrating "sufficient financial resources in the form of capital, net worth, and liquidity" per O.C.G.A. § 7-1-1022.
- Note a relevant statutory exemption: a person who purchases or holds closed mortgage loans solely for securitization into a secondary market, holding individual loans 14 days or less and not servicing them, is exempt. [T1 — O.C.G.A. § 7-1-1001(19)] This matters for certain warehouse/secondary-market structures.

**What your NMLS number changes:**

You already hold the credential that represents the single highest barrier for most would-be non-QM mortgage participants. This has three concrete implications:

1. **Time-to-market on the mortgage side compresses.** The licensing pathway that would take a new entrant 12–24 months to even begin is, for you, already partially traversed. The build sequence can assume mortgage-side capability rather than waiting on it.

2. **The regulatory-readiness story is materially stronger for capital partners.** "The founder is a licensed mortgage professional" is a sentence that changes how a sponsor bank, a warehouse lender, or a capital partner reads the risk. It moves you from "founder with an idea" to "licensed operator building an institution."

3. **It informs product sequencing.** Because mortgage is the licensed-and-hardest piece and you already hold standing there, while **business-purpose lending (DSCR, fix-and-flip) operates under fewer consumer-protection restrictions**, you have two viable entry products rather than one. The sequencing question (which leads) becomes a strategic choice rather than a licensing-gated default.

**The three structural paths** (unchanged from the prior synthesis, now with NMLS factored in):

- **Direct lending under your own licenses** — fastest for you specifically given existing NMLS standing on the mortgage side; business-purpose lending has a lighter regulatory load.
- **Sponsor-bank / partnership model** — faster to market for products outside your current licensure, at the cost of margin and flexibility; note that several states have recently tightened licensing for third parties that market/service/collect loans under bank-partnership models. [T1 — state legislative activity via CSBS]
- **Hybrid** — partnership to launch, transition to direct as the portfolio and membership base build. The path most modern lenders have followed.

## 7. What this read establishes, and what it doesn't

**Establishes (defensible with the sourcing above):**
- The segment is large: ~16.6M self-employed Americans, ~33M business owners, a $239B non-QM mortgage market growing ~10%+ annually. [T1/T2]
- The segment is measurably underserved, with the mechanism (DTI/documentation mismatch) identified in federal data rather than asserted. [T1/T2]
- Georgia/Atlanta is an unusually strong launch geography: fastest-growing non-QM market, densest Black-owned-business concentration, documented local capital gap, nonprofit-scale incumbent response. [T1/T2]
- The competitive whitespace (depth + community + development) is genuinely unoccupied. [T2/T3]
- Your NMLS standing materially improves both time-to-market and the capital-readiness narrative. [T1]

**Does not establish (and the Keena update should say so plainly):**
- That *your specific network* will convert to membership — that's what the survey proves.
- The precise size of the "sophisticated millennial / seasoned Gen-Z misbanked operator" sub-segment — the federal data captures self-employed and minority-owned business owners, but not your exact psychographic cut. The survey refines this.
- Unit economics, default behavior, or portfolio performance — those come only from operating data.
- Anything about specific capital partners' appetite — that's relationship work, not market data.

**The honest framing for Keena:** "The federal data confirms the segment is large and underserved, and Georgia is an exceptional launch market. What it can't tell us is whether *our* community will convert and how *our* portfolio will perform. That's exactly what the founding-member survey is designed to produce — and why the survey is the build, not just research about the build."
